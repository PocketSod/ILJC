

**INDY LANGUAGE JUSTICE**

**COOPERATIVE**

A Worker-Owned Language Justice Cooperative

for Indianapolis and Marion County

Business Plan and Program Design

2026

# **Vision**

The Indy Language Justice Cooperative (ILJC) is a worker-owned cooperative that creates sustainable livelihoods for language workers in Indianapolis and Marion County while advancing language justice across every dimension of community life. 

We envision a multilingual Indianapolis where all languages are respected, language work is valued and fairly compensated, and all people can fully participate in civic, social, and economic life in the languages that make them who they are or what the Community Language Cooperative in Denver calls *the language of their heart.*

Language justice, as Antena writes, is about building “a world with room for multiple languages to operate at all levels of society: from the kitchen table to the community meeting to the art museum to the City Council.” It is not about giving more people access to the status quo. It is about transforming institutions and spaces so that linguistic democracy, the equal standing of all languages, becomes the norm, not the exception.

We follow Sins Invalid in understanding that language justice is disability justice. People communicate in a multitude of ways: through spoken language, through movement, through signs, through breathing, through electronics, through giving touch, through writing, through silence. All types of communication are valid. Our cooperative honors this breadth and refuses to reduce language to spoken words alone.

# **Mission**

The Indy Language Justice Cooperative creates multilingual spaces that foster linguistic democracy. We provide high-quality interpretation, translation, training, cultural brokering, and language justice consulting to organizations, government agencies, nonprofits, and community groups across Indianapolis and central Indiana. We create a sustainable livelihood for language workers, especially those from languages of limited diffusion communities, who have historically performed this labor for free or for poverty wages. And we advance language justice by building the capacity of communities and institutions to operate multilingually, to de-center English, and to value every language equally.

# **Values**

* **Language is power:** When we express ourselves in the languages that help make us who we are, we access the full depth of our thoughts, our histories, and our possibilities. Language justice disrupts the dominance of English and builds spaces where no language has supremacy over another.

* **Nothing about us without us:** The cooperative is owned and governed by its worker-owners AKA the language workers themselves. Decisions about the cooperative’s direction, pricing, services, and values are made collectively by the people who do the work.

* **Languages of limited diffusion first:** Communities with the fewest existing language services receive our deepest investment. Chin, Karen, Kinyarwanda, Kirundi, Somali, Amharic, Haitian Creole, Indigenous Latin American languages, and other LLDs are centered, not added on.

* **Language justice is disability justice:** We honor the full range of human communicationspoken, signed, written, tactile, electronic, embodied. We commit to ASL access, plain language, and multimodal communication as core practices, not accommodations.

* **Language justice is racial justice:** Language oppression is racial oppression. English-only policies, the suppression of Indigenous languages, and the exploitation of bilingual labor are tools of white supremacy. Our work names these systems and works to dismantle them.

* **Fair compensation for language labor:** Bilingual people, especially women of color, immigrants, and refugees, have been expected to provide language labor for free. We reject this. Language work is skilled, emotionally demanding, and essential. It deserves a living wage.

* **Community self-determination:** We build on the mutual aid networks, cultural organizations, and grassroots structures that already exist. We resource and amplify community power. We do not displace it.

* **Courage and humility:** As tilde writes, “To transform language culture and practice takes time, courage, humility, and commitment.” We enter this work knowing others have paved the way and that we are always learning.

# **Legal Structure and Governance**

## **Cooperative Type**

The ILJC is organized as a worker-owned cooperative under Indiana law. Worker-owners are the people who do the language work: interpreters, translators, trainers, consultants, and cultural brokers. The cooperative is based on consensus decision making. Major decisions about services, pricing, new members, values, and strategic direction are made collectively.

## **Membership**

* **Worker-Owners:** Full members of the cooperative who have completed a provisional membership period (minimum 6 months), demonstrated competence in their language work, committed to the cooperative’s values, and been approved by the existing membership. Worker-owners share in governance, profits, and decision-making.

* **Provisional Members:** New language workers who are in their trial period. They participate in the cooperative’s work and training but do not yet have voting rights or profit-sharing. This period is for mutual assessment: the cooperative evaluates fit, and the provisional member evaluates whether the cooperative is right for them.

* **Contract Workers:** Language workers who take on specific projects but are not members of the cooperative. The cooperative maintains relationships with a broader network of language workers for languages and specializations beyond the capacity of its membership.

## **Governance Structure**

* **General Assembly:** All worker-owners meet quarterly to make major decisions, set strategic direction, approve budgets, and admit new members. Meetings are multilingual with simultaneous interpretation.

* **Coordinating Committee:** A rotating committee of 3–5 worker-owners handles day-to-day operations, scheduling, client relationships, and financial management between General Assembly meetings.

* **Finance Committee:** Following tilde’s example, a dedicated committee manages bookkeeping, invoicing, pricing, and financial reporting. This was a critical lesson from tilde’s experience: having people passionate about operations is as important as having people passionate about language justice.

* **Training and Quality Committee:** Manages interpreter and translator skill development, peer mentorship, quality assurance, and onboarding of provisional members.

* **Community Advisory Circle:** Carries forward the Community Advisory Council structure from the Bilingual Connectors program. LEP community members, partner organizations, and allies advise the cooperative on community needs, service gaps, and accountability. This circle does not govern the cooperative but holds it accountable to its community mission.

## **Bylaws and Operating Agreement**

The cooperative will invest significant time in developing thorough operating bylaws before launch, following the example of tilde, which found that taking their time with bylaws allowed them to “clarify their values and codify what it looked like to live those values as a cooperative.” The bylaws will address membership requirements and processes, decision-making procedures, profit distribution, conflict resolution, member departure and removal, and the cooperative’s relationship to the broader community.

Technical assistance for bylaw development will be sought from cooperative development organizations, including the Indiana Cooperative Development Center, the Democracy at Work Institute, and sibling language justice cooperatives.

# **Services**

## **Interpretation**

The cooperative provides simultaneous and consecutive interpretation for community meetings, government hearings, public engagement events, legal proceedings, medical appointments, school conferences, nonprofit programming, and any setting where people need to communicate across languages. Interpretation is offered in-person and remotely (Zoom, phone).

Following the language justice model pioneered by Antena and practiced by CLC, the cooperative offers two-way simultaneous interpretation as its standard \- everyone who is not bilingual wears a headset, the community’s language is centered, and English speakers receive interpretation rather than the reverse. This is the practice that flattens power: when English speakers put on the headset, the dynamic in the room shifts.

The cooperative maintains its own simultaneous interpretation equipment (transmitters, receivers, headsets, microphones) and trains all worker-owners in equipment setup, testing, and maintenance.

## **Translation**

Written translation of documents, publications, websites, forms, educational materials, outreach content, and any text that needs to reach multilingual communities. The cooperative prioritizes culturally adapted translation over word-for-word rendering, what the promotora/community health tradition calls “transcreation,” ensuring that translated materials resonate culturally, not just linguistically.

The cooperative is especially focused on translation into and from languages of limited diffusion, where commercial vendors are unavailable or unreliable. Community-based translators from within LLD communities are the cooperative’s core translation workforce.

## **Language Justice Training and Consulting**

This is where the cooperative’s community roots give it a unique advantage. Drawing on the CPC Language Justice Curriculum, the CCHE Language Justice Toolkit, Antena’s How to Build Language Justice guide, and the cooperative’s own lived experience, ILJC offers:

* **Language Justice Workshops:** Interactive trainings for organizations, government agencies, nonprofits, and coalitions on language justice principles and practices. What language justice is, how it differs from language access, how to de-center English, how to create functional multilingual spaces, and how to build organizational commitment to linguistic equity.

* **Interpreter Training:** Building on CPC’s model of “homegrown” interpreter development, the cooperative trains community members in simultaneous and consecutive interpretation, interpreter ethics, recovery techniques, working with emotions, and the specific skills needed for community organizing, social justice, and government contexts. The training follows CPC’s popular education approach: welcoming and warm-up, analysis, practice, and closing.

* **Multilingual Space Design:** Consulting with organizations to design events, meetings, conferences, and ongoing programs as functional multilingual spaces, from equipment setup to facilitation to agenda design to follow-up.

* **Organizational Language Justice Assessments:** Using the CCHE Language Justice Organizational Continuum as a framework, the cooperative helps organizations assess where they are on the spectrum from monolingual/English-dominant to fully multilingual, and develops action plans for moving toward language justice.

* **Language Access Plan Development:** Helping government agencies, nonprofits, health systems, and educational institutions develop, implement, and evaluate language access plans that go beyond compliance to embrace language justice.

## **Cultural Brokering and Community Navigation**

Carrying forward the Bilingual Connectors model, the cooperative offers cultural brokering and community navigation services on a contract basis to organizations that need ongoing, embedded support for engaging with linguistically diverse communities. This is distinct from interpretation. It is relationship-based, holistic, and focused on bridging cultural and systemic gaps, not just linguistic ones.

## **Facilitation**

Multilingual meeting and event facilitation. The cooperative provides facilitators who can guide conversations across languages and cultures, ensuring that every participant can engage meaningfully regardless of the language they speak.

## **ASL and Disability Communication Access**

Honoring Sins Invalid’s call that language justice is disability justice, the cooperative integrates ASL interpretation and disability communication access into its service model. This may involve direct ASL interpretation by worker-owners who are skilled in ASL, partnerships with Deaf community interpreters, and consultation on communication access for people who use augmentative and alternative communication (AAC), tactile communication, or other modalities.

# **Pricing Model**

The cooperative uses a tiered pricing structure modeled on the Community Language Cooperative’s approach, which maintains separate rate structures for different types of clients.

| Tier | Who | Approach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Grassroots Rate | Community organizations, mutual aid groups, grassroots coalitions, unfunded or minimally funded groups, individual community members | Sliding scale or deeply discounted. Some pro bono work built into annual budget. This is where the cooperative’s mission lives most visibly. |
| Nonprofit / Foundation Rate | Nonprofits, foundations, universities, health systems, schools | Standard professional rates. Competitive with commercial vendors but with higher quality, community-rooted service, and language justice expertise. |
| Government / Institutional Rate | City-County government, state agencies, courts, federal grantees | Full professional rates. These clients have budgets for language services and should pay accordingly. Revenue from this tier subsidizes grassroots work. |
| Corporate Rate | For-profit businesses, developers, corporations | Premium rates. Private sector clients pay the most, reflecting both the market value of the services and the cooperative’s commitment to redistributive economics. |

The tiered model ensures that the communities with the greatest need can always access services, while institutional and corporate clients provide the revenue that sustains the cooperative. Revenue from higher-tier clients directly cross-subsidizes grassroots and community work.

## **Expenses and Profit Distribution**

The cooperative’s expense structure prioritizes worker-owner compensation. Language workers are paid per-project or per-hour rates that reflect the skilled nature of their work. After expenses (equipment maintenance, insurance, marketing, administrative costs, and a reserve fund), remaining surplus is distributed among worker-owners based on the volume of work each contributed \- a patronage dividend model common in worker cooperatives.

The cooperative also maintains a Language Justice Community Fund, a set-aside from annual revenue (target: 5–10%) dedicated to pro bono language services for grassroots organizations, community interpreter training for non-members, and language justice movement-building.

# **Training and Workforce Development**

## **From Connector to Worker-Owner**

The Bilingual Connectors program provides the training pipeline for the cooperative. Connectors who are interested in professional language work enter the cooperative’s Interpreter and Translator Development Track, which provides:

* **Advanced Interpretation Skills:** Building on CPC’s curriculum, covering recovery techniques, role and ethics, vocabulary building, creating multilingual spaces, dealing with emotions, race and ethnic identity in interpretation, and queering and ungendering language.

* **Translation Fundamentals:** Written translation skills, cultural adaptation, style guides, quality assurance, and working with community review processes.

* **Cooperative Business Skills:** Financial literacy, bookkeeping, pricing, client management, marketing, and cooperative governance. Following tilde’s lesson that someone in the cooperative needs to take business classes and bring what they learn back to the group.

* **Mentorship:** Each provisional member is paired with an experienced worker-owner or with a mentor from a sibling cooperative (CLC, tilde, Cenzontle) for ongoing guidance.

## **Community Interpreter Training (Open)**

The cooperative also offers open community interpreter trainings, not limited to cooperative members,to build the broader ecosystem of language justice capacity in Indianapolis. These trainings follow the CPC model: 10 sessions, each 1.5–2 hours, grounded in popular education, and open to anyone who is bilingual and interested in interpreting in a social justice context. The cooperative views this as movement-building work, not competition—the more trained interpreters exist in the community, the stronger the language justice ecosystem becomes.

## **Ongoing Practice and Quality**

Monthly interpreter practice sessions, peer feedback circles, and annual skill evaluations ensure that the cooperative’s work remains at the highest quality. The cooperative takes seriously the CCHE Toolkit’s warning that “bad interpretation can be worse than no interpretation”—it undermines respect for people’s language, ideas, and identity. Quality is not optional; it is an expression of the cooperative’s values.

# **Market and Community Context**

## **Demand**

Marion County has over 143,000 residents who speak a language other than English at home. Tens of thousands have limited English proficiency. The City-County’s Language Access Plan creates institutional demand for interpretation and translation services. Indiana courts report that after Spanish, Burmese and Chin languages are the second most in-demand for interpreter services statewide, followed by Punjabi, Somali, Mandarin, and Arabic. Yet commercial language service vendors in Indianapolis offer very limited capacity in most LLD languages and none in many Indigenous Latin American languages.

## **Competition**

The cooperative’s primary competitive advantages over commercial language vendors are: deep community trust and cultural knowledge that commercial vendors cannot replicate; capacity in languages of limited diffusion that vendors do not serve; language justice training and consulting services that vendors do not offer; two-way simultaneous interpretation as the standard practice (most vendors default to one-way, which reinforces English dominance); and the cooperative’s embeddedness in the communities it serves, which gives it an unmatched ability to recruit, train, and support interpreters and translators from within those communities.

The cooperative does not view other language justice organizations as competitors. It views them as siblings and allies. The cooperative will actively seek mentorship, partnership, and solidarity from CLC, tilde, Cenzontle, the Language Justice Collaborative in NYC, and other language justice cooperatives and collectives.

## **Outreach Strategy**

The cooperative’s outreach is relational, not transactional. Worker-owners are community members first. They are already in the rooms, the organizations, and the relationships where language services are needed. The cooperative’s marketing is its community presence, its quality, and its values. Word of mouth within the language justice ecosystem—and direct introductions from OEBI, CBO partners, and university contacts—will be the primary client development channels in the first two years.

# **Who We Learn From**

* **tilde Language Justice Cooperative (Durham, NC):** A majority Latine-owned, worker-owned cooperative providing interpretation, translation, training, and consulting. Founded in 2017 after years of community conversations. Taught us the importance of thorough bylaws, dedicated finance capacity, and the patience required to build a cooperative right. Includes a sibling organization, the tilde Education Fund, for training.

* **Community Language Cooperative (Denver, CO):** Worker-owned cooperative founded in 2014 from community organizing in Southwest Denver’s Westwood neighborhood. Pioneered the language justice framework of centering the community’s language. Taught us the tiered pricing model and the vision of supporting local cooperatives across the state. CLC co-founder Indira Guzman: “We want every community to have their own interpreters.”

* **Cenzontle Language Justice Cooperative (Asheville, NC):** Formed in 2017 from CPC’s interpreter training ecosystem. A sister cooperative to tilde, rooted in Western North Carolina’s language justice movement.

* **San Luis Valley Language Justice Cooperative (CO):** Two women who attended a CLC training formed their own cooperative in a rural region. Taught us that the model scales to small communities and that cooperatives can start with just two people who care.

* **Language Justice Collaborative (NYC):** Trains speakers of languages of limited diffusion as interpreters and supports worker cooperative formation. Taught us how to center LLD communities and how to build from survey data about community language needs.

* **Antena / Antena Aire (Los Angeles/Houston):** Language justice and language experimentation collaborative. Their guide How to Build Language Justice is foundational—the clearest articulation of what a multilingual space looks like and how to build one.

* **Center for Participatory Change (Asheville, NC):** Their Language Justice Curriculum is the model for our interpreter training. Ten chapters grounded in popular education, covering everything from recovery techniques to queering language to race and ethnic identity in interpretation.

* **CCHE Language Justice Toolkit:** The practical toolkit for building organizational commitment to language justice. The Organizational Continuum and event planning checklist are tools we use directly.

* **Sins Invalid:** Their statement that language justice is disability justice transformed our understanding. We honor all forms of communication and integrate disability access as a core principle, not an add-on.

* **Move to End Violence:** Their framing of language justice as disrupting privilege and colonization, challenging English dominance and Western-centered knowledge, and expanding collective analysis and imagination shapes our political commitments.

# **Commitments**

* **We de-center English.** In our meetings, our trainings, our events, and our services. English speakers wear the headset. The community’s language leads.

* **We lead with languages of limited diffusion.** In recruitment, in services, in investment. Communities with the least existing support come first.

* **We pay language workers fairly.** Living wages, fair project rates, and patronage dividends. Language work is skilled, essential, and never free.

* **We are worker-owned and democratically governed.** The people who do the work make the decisions. No outside investors. No executive salaries. Collective power.

* **We honor all forms of communication.** Spoken, signed, written, tactile, electronic, embodied. Language justice is disability justice.

* **We build the ecosystem, not just the enterprise.** We train community interpreters, mentor emerging cooperatives, share our resources, and invest in movement-building. Our success is measured by the strength of the whole language justice ecosystem, not just our own bottom line.

* **We name language oppression as racial oppression.** We do not separate these struggles. Our work is anti-racist at its core.

* **We practice courage and humility.** We are learning. We will make mistakes. We will name them, learn from them, and keep building.
